Task 1 :

Introduction to Cosmology.

You may be wondering, what is Cosmology and how does it relate to Astrophysics? Astrophysics is the study of celestial bodies, and it helps explain the unexplainable. Cosmology on the other hand studies the whole universe and lets us ask questions about the beginning and end of well, everything! For Task 1, please refer to this video to get a clearer understanding of the difference and similarities between the two.

Task 2 :

Homogeneity of the Universe.

Is space really “empty”? Well quite simply, no! The “darkness” of space is actually filled with underlying microwave band static that formed the first atoms of the universe around 13.5 billion years ago. The Big Bang is what most would refer to as the start of the universe, but what actually happened? What has stayed the same? What has changed? Watch these videos to learn about cosmic inflation and the edge of an infinite universe.

Reading :

A Student's Guide To The Mathematics of Astronomy (Unit 6.4.3, 6.4.4). Fundamental Astronomy (Unit 20.2, 20.3).

Task 3 :

How will our universe end, if at all? The theories range, but the most talked-about are Friedmann’s theory of a constant, neverending, expanding universe, and the theory that the universe will succumb to gravity and collapse into itself. I mean let's be honest, the true answer is that there will be an intergalactic war between us and all alien species, right?

Task 4 :

Big Bang Theory and The Early Universe.

Assumingly, no one alive today was present during the Big Bang, but how far can we push our uncertainties and understandings? In this task, you will learn a little more about why the Big Bang definitely happened, and the questions that humankind still ponders regarding its past existence. Before you start the video, jot down some things you already know about the Big Bang (and we don’t mean the TV show).

Reading :

Task 5 :

Dark Energy.

Anti-gravity and dark energy. These topics may look super cool, and guess what? They are! Without going into too much detail, dark energy essentially governs the fate of the universe. It helps explain the exponential expansion of the universe. Anti-gravity comes into play when we describe the hypothetical phenomenon of an object that is not affected by gravity. In this task, first watch these three videos linked, and then get thinking about this challenge question posed in the forth video :

For How many of those 100 past doublings has dark energy had any significant effect? Let’s say at least 10% of the energy density. And how for many of those infinite future doublings will regular matter and energy have a significant effect – again, at least 10% of the energy density?

Reading :

Fundamental Astronomy (Unit 20.8).
